CiRA VMaker - English Narration Prompt Template

Use this template with AI assistants (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) to generate narration scripts for your slides.


PROMPT:

I have a presentation with [NUMBER] slides. Please generate professional English narration scripts for each slide.

**Slide Content:**
[Paste your slide content here - you can describe each slide or paste the text from each slide]

**Requirements:**
1. Write conversational, engaging narration (not just reading bullet points)
2. Each slide narration should be 30-60 seconds when spoken
3. Use clear, professional language
4. Use smooth transitions between ideas without announcing slide numbers
5. Do NOT include "Slide 1:", "Slide 2:", or any slide numbering in the output
6. Include pointer markers where visual attention is needed using this format:
   {@X,Y:style} where X,Y are percentages (0-100) and style is: laser, circle, arrow, spotlight, or hand
   Example: {@50,30:circle} This is the main concept we'll explore.

**Output Format:**
Return ONLY the narration text for each slide, separated by blank lines. Do NOT include slide numbers, headers, or titles.

---

[First slide narration with optional pointer markers]

---

[Second slide narration with optional pointer markers]

---

(Continue for all slides, separated by --- between each)


EXAMPLE OUTPUT:

Welcome to a masterclass in systems design. {@50,35:circle} What you're looking at is more than a circuit diagram — it's the architect's blueprint for the quantum realm. At its heart sits the Josephson junction, the nonlinear element that makes everything possible. Over the next few slides, we'll trace the journey from fundamental physics all the way up to the engineered reality of a superconducting quantum processor.

---

To understand where we're going, it helps to appreciate where we've been. {@25,55:circle} For a century, classical computation rode an exponential wave — Moore's Law, billions of transistors, all built on the bulk properties of semiconductors. {@75,55:spotlight} But in 1982, Richard Feynman pointed out a hard truth: to simulate quantum systems efficiently, computers themselves must be built from quantum elements.

---

So what exactly is a qubit? {@30,40:circle} A classical bit is a discrete switch — zero or one, end of story. {@75,35:circle} A qubit, by contrast, lives on the continuous surface of the Bloch sphere. But here's the crucial nuance — quantum speedup doesn't come from testing all states at once. It comes from engineering constructive and destructive interference between complex probability amplitudes.


TIPS:

1. Keep sentences concise - they're easier to follow when spoken
2. Avoid jargon unless your audience is technical
3. Use pointer markers sparingly - 1-3 per slide is usually enough
4. Write as if you're explaining to a colleague, not reading a textbook
5. Use smooth transitions like "Now let's look at...", "Here's the key insight..."
6. Never say "Slide one", "On this slide", or reference slide numbers


DUAL VOICE (Optional):

To use two different voices alternating in your narration, use [1] and [2] markers:
- [1] = Primary voice (first speaker)
- [2] = Secondary voice (second speaker)

Example:
[1]Welcome everyone to today's presentation.
[2]We're excited to walk you through these findings.
[1]Let's start with the key highlights.

Note: This feature only works with English (Kokoro TTS). If not using dual voice, simply omit the markers.
