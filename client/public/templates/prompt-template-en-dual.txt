CiRA VMaker - English Narration Prompt Template (Dual Voice)

Use this template with AI assistants (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) to generate narration scripts with TWO alternating speakers.


PROMPT:

I have a presentation with [NUMBER] slides. Please generate professional English narration scripts for each slide using TWO speakers who alternate throughout the presentation.

**Slide Content:**
[Paste your slide content here - you can describe each slide or paste the text from each slide]

**Requirements:**
1. Write conversational, engaging narration as a dialogue between two speakers
2. Use [1] and [2] markers to indicate which speaker is talking:
   - [1] = First speaker (e.g., main presenter)
   - [2] = Second speaker (e.g., co-host or expert)
3. Each slide narration should be 30-60 seconds when spoken
4. Use clear, professional language
5. Use smooth transitions between ideas without announcing slide numbers
6. Do NOT include "Slide 1:", "Slide 2:", or any slide numbering in the output
7. Include pointer markers where visual attention is needed using this format:
   {@X,Y:style} where X,Y are percentages (0-100)

   **Basic styles:**
   - laser - Red laser pointer dot
   - circle - Blue circle highlight
   - arrow - Red pointing arrow
   - hand - Cartoon hand cursor

   **Animated styles (cartoon effects):**
   - bouncy - Bouncy hand that springs into view
   - sketch-circle - Hand-drawn sketchy circle that draws itself
   - sketch-arrow - Squiggly hand-drawn arrow
   - pop - Purple circle that pops in with overshoot
   - wiggle - Shaking exclamation for attention

   Example: {@50,30:sketch-circle} This is the main concept we'll explore.

**Output Format:**
Return ONLY the narration text for each slide, separated by blank lines. Use [1] and [2] markers to switch speakers. Do NOT include slide numbers, headers, or titles.

---

[First slide narration with voice markers and optional pointer markers]

---

[Second slide narration with voice markers and optional pointer markers]

---

(Continue for all slides, separated by --- between each)


EXAMPLE OUTPUT:

[1]Welcome everyone to today's deep dive into quantum computing architecture.
[2]We're excited to walk you through the fundamentals of how these remarkable machines actually work.
[1]{@50,35:sketch-circle} What you're looking at here is the heart of a quantum processor — the Josephson junction. This tiny component is what makes everything possible.

---

[1]To understand where we're going, let's take a moment to appreciate where we've been.
[2]{@25,55:bouncy} For over a century, classical computing has followed Moore's Law — exponential growth powered by silicon transistors.
[1]{@75,55:pop} But in 1982, Richard Feynman challenged the computing world with a profound insight.
[2]He argued that to truly simulate quantum systems, we need computers built from quantum elements themselves.

---

[1]So what exactly makes a qubit different from a classical bit?
[2]{@30,40:sketch-circle} A classical bit is straightforward — it's either zero or one, nothing in between.
[1]{@75,35:wiggle} A qubit, on the other hand, exists on this continuous surface called the Bloch sphere.
[2]And here's the key insight — quantum speedup comes from carefully engineering interference patterns between probability amplitudes.


TIPS:

1. Keep each speaker's turn concise - natural conversation flows in short exchanges
2. Give each speaker a distinct role (e.g., one explains concepts, one provides examples)
3. Use smooth handoffs between speakers like responding to what the other said
4. Avoid having one speaker dominate - balance the dialogue
5. Use pointer markers sparingly - 1-3 per slide is usually enough
6. Never say "Slide one", "On this slide", or reference slide numbers
7. Use animated styles (bouncy, sketch-circle, pop, wiggle) for key moments to grab attention
8. Use basic styles (laser, circle, arrow) for standard pointing
